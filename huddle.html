01

The problem

Remote design teams default to a meeting for anything that needs nuance — a screen with three "not quite right" states, a flow that reads fine in a ticket but feels off when clicked through. But scheduling across time zones is slow, and most of a 30-minute review call is spent just pointing at things.

Existing async tools (Loom, screen-recording plugins) solved recording, but not the conversation that follows — feedback stayed as scattered comments disconnected from the exact moment in the video it referred to.

02

What teams actually needed

◐

Feedback tied to the frame

A comment about "the spacing at 0:14" is useless once decoupled from that exact moment. Every note needed to live on the timeline, not in a separate thread.

◑

Watch once, respond anytime

Teams wanted the async-ness to be real — no pressure to reply live, but a clear signal of who's watched and who still owes a response.

◒

Low friction to start

The tool had to beat "just open a Zoom" in speed. If recording and sharing took more than two clicks, people reverted to old habits.

04

Key design decisions

01

Comments live on the scrubber, not a sidebar

Markers pinned directly to the timeline make feedback spatially tied to the moment it describes — no re-explaining "at the part where..."
02

"Awaiting reply" as a first-class status

Async only works if it's visible who owes what. This status replaces the anxiety of "did they see this?" that drove people back to live calls.
03

Record-and-share in one motion

No separate upload/share step — recording auto-attaches to the project it was opened from, removing the friction that made Zoom feel faster.

Reflection

The interesting design problem here wasn't video — it was status. Async tools live or die on whether people trust that their feedback will actually be seen. Most of my iteration went into small, unglamorous signals: read receipts, reply badges, gentle nudges — not the recorder itself.

If I extended this, I'd want to design for the moment a thread should escalate back to a live call — async shouldn't mean never talking, just not defaulting to it.
